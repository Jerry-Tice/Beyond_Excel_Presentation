S1 — Block 2
The four failure modes
Why problems keep coming back

Jumping to solutions

The team skips problem definition and implements a fix immediately. The fix addresses the symptom — not the cause. The problem returns.

Fixing the symptom, not the cause

Root cause analysis is shallow or skipped entirely. The visible problem disappears temporarily, but the underlying condition that created it remains.

Wrong ownership model

One person owns the problem instead of a cross-functional team. Critical knowledge from maintenance, quality, or operations is missing from the solution.

No validation

The team assumes the fix worked. No metric. No timeframe. No confirmation. Six months later, the problem is back and no one knows why.

S1 — Quick Check

A packaging line was losing 45 minutes per shift to a recurring jam. Maintenance found a worn guide rail, replaced it, and the problem went away. Four months later — same jam, same location.

Which failure mode does this represent? Take a few answers from the group, then reveal.
Why C and D — both matter

C is correct but incomplete. Maintenance fixed the worn guide rail — the visible symptom — without asking why the rail was wearing in the first place. The root cause was never identified, so the same failure mode re-emerged four months later. That is a D4 failure.

D is also true. Even if they had found the right root cause, they never validated that the fix held. No confirmation step, no monitoring, no proof. That is a D6 failure.

This scenario skipped two disciplines in the arc — and that is exactly what 8D is designed to prevent. C without D is an incomplete fix. D without C is validating the wrong thing.

S1 — Block 3
Building the 8D arc
Each discipline feeds the next — skip one, and the chain breaks
D1
Team Formation
Assemble the right cross-functional team. The right people catch what one person misses.
D2
Problem Description
Define the problem precisely using Is/Is Not analysis. You cannot solve what you haven't described.
D3
Containment Actions
Stop the bleeding immediately. Containment is temporary and intentional — not a workaround you forget.
D4
Root Cause Analysis
Use 5 Whys and Fishbone to find verified root cause — not the most probable guess.
D5
Corrective Actions
Design the fix for the root cause. Not the symptom — the cause.
D6
Validate the Fix
Prove the corrective action worked — with data, over time. Not assumed. Proved.
D7
Prevent Recurrence
Update SOPs, work instructions, and training so the problem cannot come back. Most teams quit here.
D8
Recognize the Team
Close the loop. Recognition signals what the organization values and sustains CI culture.

S1 — Block 5 Exercise
Identify your facility project
Use the problem-scoping checklist in your workbook — 8 minutes, then we share
1

Real and current

An operational problem that exists right now at your facility. Not hypothetical. Not already solved.

2

Measurable

There is data or observable evidence — frequency, duration, cost, defect count, or downtime minutes you can track.

3

Within your influence

You have the authority or access to work on this problem at your facility level — not a corporate initiative.

4

Meaningful

If solved permanently, it would matter — to production, quality, safety, or cost. Not a pet project. A real headache.

Common pushback: 'We already know the fix.' Response: If it's already fixed and stayed fixed, it's not a project. If it keeps coming back — that's your project.
