Pre-Session Setup

Complete before the all-hands call starts
⚑ Three Presenters, One Hour, No Slack in the Schedule

This session runs three presenters — Jerry, Zach, and Patrick's team — across five case studies at 8 minutes each, plus a 10-minute open Q&A. That's 40 + 10 = 50 minutes of core content, with roughly 10 minutes split across the open and close. There is no buffer built in. If any single case study runs long, it comes out of Q&A time, not out of another presenter's slot.

Confirm with Zach and Patrick's team beforehand that they know their exact time budget and have rehearsed to it once.

Materials Checklist
  • Beyond Excel deck loaded and tested — all 10 slides advance correctly, speaker notes toggle works
  • Live tool links tested end-to-end: 8D Tracker App, Session 1 Training Deck (GitHub Pages), Decamarx Data Capture App, Synovate LineBeat Help Desk
  • Two browser tabs pre-staged for the Slide 4 demo: Session 1 training deck + personal 8D Tracker, ready to flip between live
  • Anthropic API key ready to paste into the Session 1 deck's Problem Statement Builder — "Polish with AI" and grading won't work without it
  • Zach briefed on his story arc, has his moment picked, screenshot/slide ready
  • Patrick's team briefed on their story arc, quote or slide content confirmed if available
  • Runtime confirmed with all three presenters: 60 minutes, 8 min per case study, 10 min Q&A

Before You Begin — Know These
  • The five case studies build in sophistication on purpose — 8D System, then Zach, then Patrick, then MW360, then the Help Desk. Simplest and most emotionally accessible first, most sophisticated last, right before the ask. Don't reorder on the fly even if it feels like a story would land better moved.
  • Slide 4 (8D System) is aimed at the "trainer" personality in the room — anyone who runs workshops or facilitates training. The interactivity is the hook. Let the live click-through worksheets and AI Review do the work rather than describing them in the abstract.
  • The Help Desk slide status is an honest, in-progress pilot — 16 users invited at Rustic Bakery, two of four deliverables complete, full end-to-end test still pending. Do not round this up to "done" if asked directly.
  • MW360's RPA-into-ERP step exists because the client's ERP has no API. Call that out explicitly — it's the detail that makes the story credible as engineering, not a demo reel.
  • This presentation itself was built the same way as the training deck. Land that meta point once, on Slide 4. Don't repeat it elsewhere — it loses force with repetition.

The Training Deck — Say This

SLIDE 7

MW360 Connect: A CI Project That Became a ProductJerry

Case Study 4 | 0:28 → 0:36
Clock
0:28 → 0:36
Duration
8 minutes
Activity
Walk the five-step pipeline left to right
Keep it at the "what it does" level — the RPA-because-no-API point is worth pausing on. It shows this isn't a green-field system, it's engineered around what the client already has.
Walk the Pipeline — Say This
  • 1. Order In: online quoter fires a webhook with a JSON order package — no manual entry at intake.
  • 2. Estimator Review: a person reviews and approves the order — the human checkpoint before anything touches the ERP.
  • 3. RPA into ERP: approval triggers RPA to enter the order into the ERP, because the platform has no API. This replaces manual keying entirely.
  • 4. AutoCAD Assembly: the app selects the right template(s) and populates order-specific details across every product ordered.
  • 5. Engineering Review: finished drawings route to engineering for final sign-off before anything ships.
If Someone Asks
"This sounds fully automated — are people getting replaced?"
No — there are two human checkpoints in the pipeline, estimator and engineering, and both remain. The automation removed manual re-keying and drawing assembly, not judgment. That's worth saying explicitly if it comes up; don't let the room assume otherwise.

How to Run It
  • Jerry moderates and calls on questions. Technical questions about a specific build get redirected to whoever presented it — don't let Jerry answer for Zach or Patrick's team if they're in the room and better positioned to answer.
  • Watch the clock. At 0:54, move to the close regardless of whether hands are still up — offer to take remaining questions after the call ends.
Likely Question
"Isn't this just a fancier PowerPoint?"
No — PowerPoint can't take input, score it, or run logic. Every build shown today does something a static deck or spreadsheet structurally cannot: capture data, apply AI judgment, or trigger a downstream action.
Likely Question
"What happens when the vibe-coded app breaks and the person who built it isn't around?"
Fair concern, worth taking seriously rather than deflecting. Answer honestly: these are early-stage tools, not hardened enterprise software, and that tradeoff is explicit — speed now, with a plan to formalize anything that becomes business-critical. Don't claim more production-readiness than the tools actually have.
Likely Question
"Do we need IT or security sign-off before using something like this with a client?"
Yes, for anything touching client data or systems — that's not optional, and saying so directly builds credibility rather than undermining the pitch. Point to whatever the firm's actual review process is rather than improvising one on the spot.
Likely Question
"What did this actually cost the firm, in your time?"
Answer with the real numbers you have — the 8D Tracker's ~4 hours is the sharpest data point in the deck. Where you don't have a clean number (Zach's build, Patrick's team's hours), say so rather than estimating on the spot.
